The Figma Compiler: Overview

The ultimate indie dev weapon. Convert raw Figma designs into pixel-perfect React code in seconds.

The "Screenshot-to-Code" Illusion

Many platforms brag about "Screenshot-to-Code." It looks great in Twitter demos, but in reality, vision models hallucinate. They guess margin sizes, they hallucinate random hex colors, and they completely fail to understand responsive Auto-Layout constraints. The result is a brittle UI that breaks the moment you resize the window.

We built the Gor://a Figma Compiler to solve this permanently. We don't just look at a picture of your design; we read the actual mathematics behind it.

How Our AST Engine Works

When you provide a Figma link, Gor://a does not just send it to a vision model. Instead, our backend performs a Smart Fidelity Compression:

  1. Node Extraction: The engine connects to the Figma API and downloads the raw JSON tree of your specific design frame.
  2. Smart Compression (The AST): Raw Figma JSON is massive (often millions of characters). Our Python backend strips out invisible layers, mathematical vectors, and bloated metadata. It extracts only the crucial Tailwind-compatible data: layoutMode, itemSpacing, padding, typography, and hex fills.
  3. The Zero-Shot Compilation: This highly compressed Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) is sent to our Gemini Flash compiler, which translates the strict mathematical rules directly into a single, massive React TSX component.
  4. Asset Injection: Simultaneously, the engine asks Figma to render the frame as a high-quality JPG, converts it to Base64, and saves it to your database as your project's visual snapshot.

Massive Token Savings: Because we compress the Figma data before sending it to the LLM, we save you tens of thousands of tokens per generation. You get a mathematically perfect UI, and your free token balance barely takes a hit.

The Result

When the WebContainer boots up, your App.tsx is already fully populated with the exact colors, padding, and Flexbox layouts you defined in Figma. The AI Swarm is then notified that a design was imported, and it stands by, ready to help you wire up the backend logic and interactivity.
